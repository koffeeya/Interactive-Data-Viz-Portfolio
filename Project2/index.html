Why Are Tribal Schools Struggling to Meet Student Needs?
Tribal schools are an important means to preserve and pass on the distinct cultures of Native American people.
But young people in tribal schools, particularly vulnerable populations, face immense challenges in keeping pace with their non-Native peers in academic outcomes. Their schools struggle to find the basic resources, staff, and funding to adequately meet their unique needs.
ABOUT TRIBAL SCHOOLS
There are approximately 2.4 million students who identify as "American-Indian / Alaska Native" in the United States. 93% of these students attend regular public schools. The remaining 7% (about 40,000 students) attend one of 184 tribal schools.

These schools are vitally important to undo the traumatic legacy of Native American education in the United States.

According to the National Indian Education Association, tribal schools are a "recognition of tribal sovereignty" and grounded in "Native epistemology â€” Native ways of knowing and believing".
The Bureau of Indian Education (BIE) oversees tribal schools and colleges, and in some cases directly operates them. The majority (70%) are still directly controlled by the tribe or nation, and pass on the unique culture, identity, and language of their communities.

However, tribal schools face immense and long-standing challenges in meeting the needs of their students. From a lack of basic internet access to teacher shortages to crumbling infrastructure, these challenges have a direct impact on the outcomes of Native young people, particularly those in vulnerable populations.

OUTCOMES
Tribal schools must still meet national standards for academic achievement. But students in tribal schools are falling behind, particularly vulnerable populations.

According to 2015-2016 tribal school report cards (the most recent data available from the BIE), only 12 schools out of 137 had a majority (50% or more) of students test "proficient" in math, and 26 schools proficient in reading.

By far, the widest gap is seen in the population of students with disabilities. Only 1 school had the majority of students with disabilities test proficient in reading, and 2 in math.

CHALLENGES
Students have to travel far: The average tribal school bus drives 479 miles per day to get students to school, compared to 74 miles per day nationally (according to one study).
